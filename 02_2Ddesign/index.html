Part I: Designing and Creating a Toolbox

In 2D: On Fusion 360

I created a box inspired by the Japanese art of sashimono , a joinery technique that use wood joints instead of nails. The box — and furniture that uses sashimono — is thus able to last longer, often has stronger joints, but is also easier to take apart and transport. I was particularly inspired by this design, by @chrisinottawa on the Lumberjocks website. It uses a sliding lid and tabs on its panels that fit snugly in the slots.

I first sketched out the panels of the box and the dimensions according to a general shoebox size.

Here, you can replicate the dimensions:

Following this, I started to enter all the dimensions into Fusion 360 and created the panels. For each panel, I created a 2D sketch of the associated rectangle and extruded it with the thickness of cardboard. For the slots on the Long Side Panel, I layered on a 2D square and extruded it through the cardboard, repeating this five more times. On the Short Side Panel, I created tabs that corresponded with the slots. Additionally, I drew “This is Joanna’s” on Procreate and turned the image into an SVG file, which vectorizes the image, allowing it to cut through panels. Here, you can see my progress.
As you can see from the video, I struggled with learning how to turn everything into a sketch to prepare it to be sent to Rhino. Luckily, with the help of the CAs and my peers, I simply turned each individual panel into a DXF file. That is definitely something I still need to clarify for myself.


In 3D: Assembling the box

I finally printed everything using the Laser Printer!

The cardboard, while not wood, allowed the tabs to fit perfectly in the slots.
I assembled everything else using hot glue. I added handles onto the sliding lid and here is the final result!
